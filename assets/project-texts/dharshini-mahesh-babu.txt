TITLE: Room for More
ADVISOR: Samia Henni

Over the course of the twentieth century, Canada has been shaped by mass migration, carrying with it lives, memories, and deeply rooted practices. For many immigrants, culinary traditions offer more than sustenance, as they provide a form of ontological security that holds together fragments of continuity across distance and displacement. The food axis, encompassing spaces of preparation, storage, and service, becomes a powerful site of agency and placemaking. For the South Indian community in particular, Canadian domestic spaces often fall short of supporting these practices, producing a tension that calls for adaptation. What emerges is not simply adjustment but expansion, as cooking spills into balconies, living rooms, and borrowed spaces, shaped by values of commensality that have always assumed a community rather than a bounded household. This project begins with that overflow and understands it as both necessity and possibility. It asks how South Indian immigrants in the Greater Toronto Area inscribe their migrant sensibilities into the food axis, and how this ongoing act of inscription shapes their sense of belonging. The food axis is not a background condition of immigrant life, but a space dense with emotion and meaning, carrying love, joy, and at times grief, while also marking family success, the negotiation of roles, and the making of home in a place that is still becoming familiar.

The proposal takes form as a hybrid cultural food hall and community centre organized around a central courtyard, drawn from the spatial logic of agraharam housing in South India. In the agraharam, rows of homes face inward toward a shared street and temple, forming a spatial and social fabric grounded in collective life, ritual, and proximity. This project reinterprets that logic, allowing the courtyard to become a contemporary gathering ground where boundaries soften and everyday life unfolds in relation to others. Every space in the building, including the collective kitchen, gathering hall, multifaith room, event space, and workshop and teaching areas, is oriented toward this shared centre. The design is expressed through narrative storytelling graphics that move across scales, from the body to the kitchen, from the courtyard to the city, tracing how everyday acts accumulate into larger spatial and cultural forms, and how belonging is not given, but continuously made. Ultimately, this project proposes a typology that can serve not only immigrant communities but anyone who shares values of commensality, while positioning adaptive reuse as a vital model for sustaining cultural continuity.
