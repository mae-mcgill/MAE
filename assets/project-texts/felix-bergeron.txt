TITLE: Beyond the Building
ADVISOR: Naomi Keena

According to the UNEP (2023), the buildings and construction sector accounts for approximately 37% of global greenhouse gas emissions. In response, architects occupy a critical position. Through their choices of materials, assemblies, and systems, they shape the energetic, and territorial configuration of the built environment. Yet, in contemporary practice, these decisions are largely driven by constraints of cost, time, and performance, often relegating the ecological and social implications to a secondary role. This condition is embedded within a globalized model of production in which materials are extracted, processed, and transported across planetary scales, dissociating their origin from their implementation. As highlighted in Heterogeneous Constructions, this disconnection results from a historical process of industrialization that has progressively erased regional construction logics in favor of homogenized practices. Contemporary buildings thus tend to become abstract assemblies, disconnected from the resources, knowledge systems, and territories that make them possible.

In response, this research is grounded in the framework of construction ecology, an approach that considers architecture as a system of material, energetic, and human flows. The methodology unfolds in two complementary phases. First, the analysis of a case study located in Quebec allows for the mapping of supply chains, material transformations, and the actors involved in the building’s production. Second, a design proposal focused on reuse extends this analysis by exploring how these logics can inform contemporary design decisions. Specifically, it investigates the adaptive reuse of the Église Saint-Édouard de Port-Alfred (1927–1928), transforming it into a public library.

Existing buildings, notably those with heritage value, emerge as relevant resources. Unlike most contemporary constructions, they embody situated material logics rooted in specific local contexts. Their transformation thus becomes a projective strategy, enabling architecture to re-engage with its territory without mobilizing new large-scale processes of extraction and production. This is especially significant in Quebec, where nearly 28% of churches are now underused, abandoned or demolished (Lux, 2025) raising critical questions about their future. Their adaptive reuse offers an opportunity not only to conserve material and cultural resources, but also to reinvent these spaces as infrastructures for public life and collective engagement.

This research proposes a shift in architectural thinking: from the building as product to the building as process, from material as product to material as relation. It also challenges a still-dominant posture in which architectural intervention can emerge from a singular formal gesture. Instead, it suggests that architectural quality can emerge from a careful reading of the existing, where doing less, becomes a critical position in response to contemporary challenges.
