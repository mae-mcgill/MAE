TITLE: Building on Unstable Ground: Climate-Adaptive Housing for Northern Landscapes
ADVISOR: Michael Jemtrud

Northern communities in Nunavik face a complex convergence of environmental, social, and infrastructural challenges driven by permafrost thaw, rapid population growth, and persistent housing shortages. Rising Arctic temperatures are destabilizing ice-rich soils, compromising traditional construction systems and increasing the vulnerability of infrastructure. At the same time, Nunavik is experiencing a severe housing crisis characterized by overcrowding, long waiting lists, and aging buildings often affected by mold and structural damage caused by shifting ground conditions. These pressures are further intensified by geographic isolation, high construction costs, limited buildable land, and reliance on southern supply chains. The region currently faces a deficit of more than 800 housing units, with its population projected to increase by approximately 36% by 2041. Concurrently, climate change is accelerating environmental instability across the region. Climate projections indicate that mean annual temperatures in Nunavik could rise by 4–8°C by 2100, leading to deeper active layers, widespread permafrost degradation, and increased risks of landslides, erosion, and ground deformation.

The research applies a multidisciplinary methodology that combines geospatial analysis and architectural design, grounded in existing climate projections and permafrost maps for Salluit to inform site-specific decisions. Building on this context, the project proposes an adaptive settlement strategy focused on expanding the existing village through culturally responsive, multigenerational housing that accommodate Inuit family patterns and ways of living while responding to changing permafrost through elevated construction systems and coordinated utility corridors beneath raised walkways, allowing buildings and infrastructure to be incrementally repositioned as conditions evolve. While the primary goal is in-situ adaptation, the same flexible architectural and infrastructural systems keep the option of gradual relocation open if certain areas become unstable, allowing homes and services to be moved with minimal disruption to community life. The final phase develops architectural prototypes and settlement models suited to Arctic logistical constraints, prioritizing prefabrication, energy efficiency, and long-term adaptability to evolving climatic and geological conditions.

This research argues for a fundamental shift in northern architecture, replacing static, permanent structures with adaptive and responsive systems capable of engaging with a changing environment. By addressing both the housing shortage and the effects of permafrost degradation, this thesis develops a framework for the long-term resilience of Arctic communities, empowering Inuit self-determination in shaping the built environment, and ensuring that future housing is not only structurally resilient and sustainable but also deeply connected to northern cultural values.
