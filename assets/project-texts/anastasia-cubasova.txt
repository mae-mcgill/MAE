TITLE: Honeys! I’m Home: Designing Living Spaces for Families Beyond the Nuclear “Norm”
ADVISOR: Annmarie Adams

Scholarship on queer architecture prioritizes gaybourhoods, public urban spaces, and nightlife over the domestic sphere. Existing research on queer domesticity focuses on precedents queered through use, where residents adapt structures to suit needs rather than inhabiting spaces intentionally designed for them. In Montreal’s housing crisis, LGBT+ communities encounter systemic barriers, including landlord discrimination and exclusion from traditional co-operatives that rely on tight referral systems. While Quebec recognizes pluriparentalité (legal plural parenthood), architectural standards continue to favour the nuclear family. Within this context, queer kinship structures and pluriparental arrangements lack supportive living environments.

Honeys! I’m Home proposes a co-operative housing model for queer and pluriparental families, through repurposing the Ethel parking garage in Verdun. While developers speculate on this property, the Comité d’action des citoyens et citoyennes de Verdun (CACV) claims the structure for mixed social housing. Presenting my research to them at one of the mobilization committees ensured the project remained grounded in local community advocacy.

Archival research at the ArQuives in Toronto and consultations with experts such as curator Jae Kim reveal a gap in documented queer domesticity. This silence demonstrates how some queer experiences are omitted from institutional records, contributing to the lack of queer domestic architectural precedents. Without recorded histories, it became important to rely on living memory and speculative tools to bridge archival silence and create a precedent. This project integrates architecture with sociology, sexology, and queer theory to capture the complex ways queer identities occupy and transform the home.

Participatory design is central to the project: designing queer, in my understanding, means designing with queers. Two workshops took place at the Centre des femmes de Verdun with four participants acting as co-authors. Participants were considered as future residents and were involved throughout different stages of the design phase. Play is central to my methodology, as it has been central to queer resistance (camp humor, Kiki Balls). Characters, collages, dollhouses, colours, and fun carried the project from beginning to end.

Other methodological inspirations include architect Daniel Ovalle Costal, who utilizes dollhouses to imagine homes for queer people and challenge building code norms, and architect Phyllis Birkby, who in the 70s created workshops pushing women to imagine fantasy environments. Visits to Amsterdam queer co-ops were used as case studies (L.A. Rieshuis, Amsterdam Hallen, and Tetterode). I also shared my research in a lecture at the ARCAM Amsterdam Architecture Centre.
