TITLE: Maljaa – Homemaking in Spaces of Inhospitality: On the Nostalgia of Beirut’s Bomb Shelters
ADVISOR: Ipek Türeli

This project examines how Beirut’s makeshift bomb shelters, improvised during the Lebanese Civil War (1975 to 1990), functioned as temporary domestic spaces and how their memory persists within a broader context of cultural erasure. Often selected instinctively based on immediate danger, these shelters became sites where families lived for extended periods, developing routines and practices that transformed spaces of fear into fragile environments of care, familiarity, and belonging. While these experiences shaped collective relationships to the city, they remain largely unspoken. In Lebanon, this silence is reinforced by political and cultural mechanisms that have discouraged the preservation of war memory, resulting in the absence of formal archives or infrastructural acknowledgment of civilian life during conflict. Today, although many of these shelters still exist within Beirut’s urban fabric, they are largely obsolete and disconnected from their original function, even as cycles of violence persist. In response, this project reconstructs the shelter as a site of domestic memory through oral histories, treating personal narratives as both architectural knowledge and design methodology. Through interviews and workshops, it explores how individuals recall inhabiting these spaces and how acts of homemaking emerged under conditions of instability. The resulting exhibition does not aim to produce a fixed or accurate reconstruction, but instead foregrounds the subjective, fragmented, and repetitive nature of memory. By re-centering the shelter as a lived domestic environment, the project challenges dominant understandings of home as stable and permanent, proposing instead that domesticity can be produced through adaptation, collective experience, and the ongoing negotiation of insecurity.
