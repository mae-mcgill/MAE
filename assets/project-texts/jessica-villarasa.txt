TITLE: Housing, Care, and the Architecture of Proximity
ADVISOR: Ipek Türeli

“KASAMA” is a Tagalog word that means with, together, included. KASAMA begins from the belief that housing is never only a container for living, but a framework through which care is carried, negotiated, and shared across time. Rooted in the lived experience of immigrant and diasporic family life in Montréal, the project asks how architecture can be designed to hold demographic change without displacing the relational structures that make everyday life possible.

The thesis begins with a domestic ethnography of my family’s fiveplex in Saint-Léonard, treated as a living laboratory for intergenerational care. Mapping the building’s occupancy across three decades revealed how a standard, repeated floor plan absorbed continuous demographic flux: relatives migrating in, children returning, elders requiring care, households overlapping across floors. Within a mostly unchanged architecture, life kept shifting. Rooms absorbed new programs. Thresholds became sites of exchange. Circulation carried groceries, childcare, repair, and elder care. What appeared ordinary revealed itself as an architecture already organized by reciprocity, operating through spatial behaviors that conventional housing drawings do not capture.

From this research, KASAMA draws three design directives. Housing must absorb demographic change without forcing displacement or renovation. Circulation must be designed beyond access, supporting visibility, adjacency, aging in place, and the informal transfers of care that sustain intergenerational households. And the kitchen must be understood as spatial capacity rather than service space, because cooking practices, bulk storage, and shared meals are structural to how diasporic households maintain collective life across unit boundaries.

These directives are tested at Domaine Choisy, a rental block in Saint-Léonard directly across from the fiveplex, where two vacant lots sit unreplaced within a block under the city’s right of pre-emption. New housing on these lots increases density, justifies shared elevator infrastructure serving new and existing buildings, and funds reinvestment in the block’s collective ground. The unit mix ranges from micro and one-bedroom apartments to intergenerational cluster units, and exterior circulation is thickened into a programmed collective spine carrying shared laundry, pantry, community kitchen, and guest rooms.

KASAMA argues that proximity is the missing design variable in Canadian housing, and that architecture can make it possible without demanding that families collapse into each other.
