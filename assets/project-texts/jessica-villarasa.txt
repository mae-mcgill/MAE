TITLE: Housing as a system of care, adaptation and time
ADVISOR: Ipek Türeli

"KASAMA" is a Tagalog word that means with, together, included. KASAMA begins from the position that housing is never only a container for living, but a framework through which care is carried, negotiated, and shared across time. Rooted in the lived experience of immigrant and diasporic family life in Montréal, the project asks how architecture can hold demographic change without displacing the relational structures that make everyday life possible.

The thesis begins with a domestic ethnography of my family's fiveplex in Saint-Léonard, treated as a living laboratory for intergenerational care. Mapping the building's occupancy across three decades revealed how a standard, repeated floor plan absorbed continuous demographic flux: relatives migrating in, children returning, elders requiring care, households overlapping across floors. Within a mostly unchanged architecture, life kept shifting. Rooms absorbed new programs. Thresholds became sites of exchange. Circulation carried groceries, childcare, repair, and elder care. What appeared ordinary revealed itself as an architecture already organized by reciprocity, operating through spatial behaviors that conventional housing drawings do not capture.

KASAMA situates this reading within a longer architecture of care discourse (Matrix, 1984; Tronto, 1993; Lacaton and Vassal, 2020), arguing that spatial elasticity is itself care infrastructure. The thesis tests this claim across three scales: a neighbourhood already operating as intergenerational territory, a block where shared infrastructure does work the private unit cannot, and a unit that absorbs the changing arrangements of a family across decades.

These scales converge at Domaine Choisy, a postwar rental block of over 400 units directly across from the fiveplex, where two vacant lots sit unreplaced within the block. Selective infill on these lots, seven storeys in mass timber, funds accessibility retrofits to the existing bar buildings: a thickened exterior gallery system, shared elevator and stair cores, and mutualized laundry, pantry, and guest rooms. The infill pays for the block. The block justifies the infill. Intergenerational corner suites share a wall, a plumbing stack, and a heavy acoustic sliding partition that opens or closes as a family reconfigures over time.

The project tracks the Santos family across three scenarios: Kasama (together), Sariling Pinto (one's own door), and Lagi Nandito (always here). KASAMA argues that proximity is the missing design variable in Canadian housing, and that architecture can make it possible without demanding that families collapse into each other.
