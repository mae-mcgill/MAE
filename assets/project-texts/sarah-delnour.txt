TITLE: Infrastructure as Curation: The Ruin as Living Archive
ADVISOR: Samia Henni

This project proposes architectural curation as a form of spatial and social infrastructure capable of intervening in the life cycles of existing buildings. Rather than limiting exhibitions to representation, it argues for site-specific architectural acts that interpret, stabilize, and activate urban environments marked by obsolescence, decay, and transformation. Central to this argument is a reframing of the ruin: not as a terminal condition, but as a productive encounter between human construction and natural processes, one that can be made legible through curatorial design.

This position emerges from a critical reassessment of conventional architectural exhibition, which remains largely constrained by institutional boundaries and controlled interiors, detached from the spatial and social realities of the city. In response, the project reframes curation as a constructive, infrastructural practice that produces spatial conditions, mediates temporal change, and participates directly in urban life. Positioned within debates on adaptive reuse, museum studies, and post-industrial landscapes, the research advances the concept of “infrastructure as curation”: a design methodology that engages material histories, negotiates processes of weathering and repair, and supports new forms of public use. Industrial ruins are understood as sites where cultural memory and latent potential converge, where traces of labor, abandonment, and decay can be preserved and interpreted rather than erased, transforming buildings into living archives of the urban fabric.

The former Canada Malting Plant in Saint-Henri, Montreal, serves as the site of investigation. Comprising 29 silos and two malting houses, this monumental industrial ruin sits within a neighborhood shaped by post-industrial decline, ongoing gentrification, and a deeply contested future. The project proposes an intervention that stabilizes and reactivates the two silo batteries and adjacent structures through curatorial strategies that embrace incompletion and partial repair. Treating the ruin as both subject and medium, new architectural insertions enable public access to the building’s unconventional vertical spaces while foregrounding material traces of decay and adaptation. A portion of the complex is preserved as a ruin park, in which processes of weathering, maintenance, and transformation remain visible as part of an evolving curatorial experience. The remainder of the intervention introduces a cultural institute comprising gallery spaces, event spaces, and studios for local artists, establishing a hybrid condition between cultural infrastructure, community use, and the interpreted ruin.

The project ultimately argues that to curate a ruin is to care for it: to hold open the tension between decay and inhabitation, and to make that tension legible as a form of public life.
