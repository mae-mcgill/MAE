TITLE: Achieving Circularity: The Urban Industrial Heritage as a Material Reservoir
ADVISOR: Michael Jemtrud

The construction industry is consuming and discarding materials at an unsustainable pace, positioning it as a critical lever for environmental transformation. According to the Government of Canada, the sector accounts for roughly 30% of raw material extraction and waste generation, while emissions embedded in building materials remain largely overlooked.

In parallel, accelerated urban redevelopment is reshaping Canadian cities under intense economic and demographic pressures. Millions of new housing units are required to restore affordability, driving rapid, large-scale projects that often prioritize speed and financial return over environmental responsibility. Montréal’s Bridge—Bonaventure district exemplifies this condition: the City plans to introduce several thousand new units by 2050 within a former industrial landscape along the Lachine Canal, placing existing infrastructures at risk of demolition and potentially erasing significant material resources and embodied carbon.

Within this context, the Pointe-du-Moulin sector, home to the Ogilvie Flour Mill, emerges as a critical testing ground. The site retains substantial industrial structures that embody valuable material reserves and cultural memory, offering a unique opportunity to reposition industrial heritage as an active public resource within a circular framework.

In this context, my thesis addresses the following question: how can material stocks embedded in industrial buildings, particularly the Ogilvie Flour Mill, be recovered, revalorized, and reintegrated through circular design strategies to support a public-driven redevelopment model with environmental, social, and economic value? Through a research-creation approach, my project aims to transform industrial “waste” into architectural value, grounding circular economy principles in a real Canadian context.

The methodology follows a four-part systemic framework: ① material stock identification, ② assessment of recovery potential, ③ multi-scalar design development, and ④ evaluation. BIM-based modelling and a Bill of Materials are employed to quantify embedded resources, while Life Cycle Assessment evaluates existing conditions and reuse strategies. Together, these methods are situated within a triple-bottom-line framework that informs a long-term transformation scenario, culminating in the conversion of the Ogilvie Flour Mill into a circularity hub for material reuse and community engagement.

While European cities have advanced urban mining strategies, Canada has yet to operationalize circular construction at architectural and urban scales. This project responds to that gap by proposing a replicable, design-driven methodology grounded in empirical analysis and systems thinking, demonstrating how industrial heritage can become a productive resource for resilient urban transformation.
