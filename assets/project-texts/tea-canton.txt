TITLE: Behind Open Doors
ADVISOR: Annmarie Adams

Behind Open Doors translates sex workers’ lived experiences into architectural space. Its spatial strategies are informed by sex worker anecdotes shared in conferences, podcasts, and interviews, where workers describe how they navigate safety in their day-to-day lives. Rather than imposing a fixed definition of safety, the project treats these lived practices as a form of spatial knowledge grounded in movement, awareness, and control.

This project does not frame sex work as inherently empowering or without risk. However, it acknowledges sex workers as agents: individuals who make decisions about their labour and their lives, even within constrained and often difficult conditions. This shift reframes sex workers not as passive subjects of policy, but as active producers of knowledge about space and survival.

Sex work has long been embedded in Montreal’s urban fabric. Yet its presence has consistently been constructed as a threat to public morality, resulting in cycles of regulation, policing, and displacement. Over the twentieth century, efforts to “clean up” the city led to the closure of many venues and the suppression of sex work’s visibility. Today, zoning and licensing restrictions continue this process, making it nearly impossible for new spaces to emerge. The result is a form of spatial erasure where sex work does not disappear, but is pushed into more hidden, fragmented, and often less safe environments.

This condition is reinforced by Canada’s current legal framework, often described as the “end-demand” model, which criminalizes many aspects surrounding sex work. While intended to reduce harm, critics argue that these laws further isolate workers, restrict access to resources, and undermine their ability to work safely. In this context, sex workers are not only marginalized within their professional environments, but also “othered” within the broader social fabric of the city, with limited access to spaces that support long-term stability and community.

In response, this project positions space as central to these conditions. Safety is not only legal or social, but spatial — shaped by access, visibility, control, and the ability to gather. By foregrounding these dimensions, Behind Open Doors calls for a rethinking of how cities accommodate, exclude, and erase sex workers, and advocates for a more equitable relationship between sex workers and the urban environment.
