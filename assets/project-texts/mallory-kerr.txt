TITLE: Evolving Forever Wild: Adapting a Hut-to-Hut Network through the Adirondack Landscape
ADVISOR: Michael Jemtrud

Located in the Adirondacks, this project imagines a hut-to-hut network within the High Peaks Wilderness area, where the architecture emerges directly from the surrounding forest. Rooted in a forest-building approach, the project links construction to the rhythm of the landscape, proposing hiking shelters that are not imposed on the land, but rather informed by it. The Adirondacks carry the traces of their past. Once heavily logged in the nineteenth century, the forest was pushed to the edge before being protected in 1892. Today, it is vast but no longer untouched, largely composed of second-growth stands, with only fragments of original forest remaining. As temperatures rise, this forested system continues to shift. Winters shorten, ecosystems migrate, and hardwood species increasingly take the place of the softwoods that once defined the region. At the same time, increasing tourism places new pressures on the land, calling for forms of access that are sensitive. To alleviate these pressures, the thesis proposes a series of small, low-impact huts that support year-round movement through the wilderness. These structures are conceived not as fixed objects, but as responses to the specific conditions of each site, its terrain, its climate, and above all, its trees. The project looks closely at the forest itself, identifying the species that inhabit each stand and those that are beginning to emerge as the ecosystem evolves.

As hardwoods become more present, the research explores how their strength, density, and character can shape new forms of construction. Moving away from a reliance on non-native softwood systems, the project considers how underutilized species might inform alternative structural systems, joinery, and spatial expression. In this way, the architecture becomes a reflection of ecological change, adapting as the forest adapts. These huts may themselves be temporal. Their lifespan is tied to what the forest can provide at a given moment, constructed from available species, they may weather, be repaired, or eventually return to the ground as conditions shift, following the movement of the forest and its changing ecosystem. In this way, the network remains in flux, shaped by cycles of growth, use, and regeneration. On-site exploration grounds the project in a lived experience. Walking the terrain, observing varying species at different elevations, and documenting the material reality of the woods. Rather than resisting change, this thesis embraces it. It positions architecture as a quiet participant in a shifting ecosystem, offering a building model that supports both local forest resilience and a more careful, attentive way of inhabiting the landscape.
