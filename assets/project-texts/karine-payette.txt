TITLE: Living together: Rethinking urban densification to actively include our seniors
ADVISOR: Michael Jemtrud

By 2041, seniors will represent more than 26% of Quebec's population, marking a profound demographic transformation that will significantly influence the way cities are planned and designed. In a context already characterized by social inequalities, limited opportunities for community participation, and an ongoing housing crisis, many seniors face increasing risks to their autonomy and well-being. These challenges call for innovative approaches that rethink how urban environments can better support aging populations while strengthening social cohesion.

In response, this research project explores how the combination of compact housing, community-oriented social hubs, and the activating potential of Montreal's ruelles can contribute to the creation of more age-friendly urban environments. Rather than considering housing as an isolated element, the project examines how these three interconnected components can work together to enhance seniors' quality of life. Through everyday social interactions, proximity-based mobility, and stronger connections to their surroundings, seniors can remain active participants in community life. This integrated approach aims to foster a sense of belonging within the existing urban fabric while promoting a collective and participatory model of inclusion. At the same time, it proposes a sustainable and human-scaled alternative to conventional forms of urban densification, supporting the development of resilient and intergenerational neighborhoods.

The research is guided by the following central question: How might targeted architectural and urban acupuncture interventions create environments that enable active seniors to age in place longer while fostering stronger community integration, connection, and vitality?

To address this question, the methodology combines literature review, field data, morphological and comparative analyses, and principles of inclusive urban design. Together, these methods provide a comprehensive understanding of seniors' social interactions, their relationship with their living environment, and the urban context in which potential interventions could be implemented. Particular attention is given to Montreal's ruelles, as well as to the neighborhood of Rosemont—La Petite-Patrie, which attracts particular interest due to the availability of space within its urban fabric and its strong social potential.

The research also highlights how Quebec—and particularly Montreal—lags behind in adopting regulatory measures that facilitate the implementation of accessory dwelling units (ADUs), despite their recognized potential to diversify housing options and support aging in place. Nevertheless, the analysis reveals the strong potential of human-scale neighborhoods where solidarity is tangible at the borough level. Ultimately, Montreal emerges as an incubator of ideas, where existing constraints can become opportunities for tailored architectural and urban solutions.
