TITLE: Places to Grow: Reimagining the Future of Farmland at the Rural–Urban Fringe
ADVISOR: Samia Henni

From Indigenous cultivation, European settlements, and the rise of the family farm to today, Ontario’s farming industry has undergone a dramatic transformation. Farming in the 21st century is increasingly shaped by industrialization and corporatization, driven by pressures for profit and developable land. This shift has endangered the future of family farms and weakened society’s connection to its food system. Mid-sized farms, between 100 and 500 acres, are caught between rising land values, unstable markets, high operating costs, and a generational gap in succession. As primary occupants of the rural–urban fringe, these properties often follow a predictable path once stewardship is lost: annexation, rezoning, and eventual subdivision. What disappears extends well beyond productive acreage, including generations of ecological knowledge, rural settlement patterns, and relationships to land that resist easy quantification.

My family’s 133-acre dairy farm is one of these properties. The McLean Farm has been in my family for over a century, sitting directly on the border between Barrie and Oro-Medonte. In early 2026, the land was annexed by the City of Barrie and is now under development pressure. With my grandfather’s passing, my grandmother’s move to a retirement home, my uncle's retirement, and no clear successor, the farm has lost the family presence that sustained it for generations. Today, the barns and farmhouse sit empty, the fields leased for cash crops, and ownership is divided as the land sits in limbo, caught between what it was and what the market expects it to become.

The McLean Farm becomes both a case study and a reflection, prompting questions about how farmland is valued, governed, and designed in times of rapid change. The challenge is not simply preservation but creating futures in which farmland can thrive. This thesis uses McLean Farm as a starting point to examine how farmland in Ontario’s rural–urban fringes can support a renewed relationship between society and agriculture in the 21st century. Based on the principles of both ecological and social revitalization, the project proposes a framework that integrates regenerative farming techniques, clustered housing organized around shared agricultural and social spaces, public programming tied to agrotourism, and a cooperative ownership model. The existing barns and farmhouses anchor the site to its history, while degraded fields are restored by reintroducing biodiversity through polyculture systems.

Ultimately, this work positions architecture as an active participant in shaping how land is valued and cared for. Rather than preserving the farm unchanged, it carries its values forward into a future where farming, living, and stewardship remain inseparable.
