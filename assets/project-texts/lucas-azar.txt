TITLE: What Remains
ADVISOR: Martin Bressani

"The historical operation in its entirety can then be considered an act of sepulchre."

— Paul Ricoeur, Memory, History, Forgetting

Long before the dike and the mills, the rapids at the Sault-au-Récollet were a portage site, a place where Indigenous peoples read the river's force and carried their vessels overland, moving with water rather than against it. In 1726, the miller Simon Sicard harnessed that same current, building a dike between the islands of Montréal and La Visitation to drive a succession of mills that would shape the industrial life of the Sault for over two centuries. What remains today are ruins, turbines stilled in their channels, and a landscape whose layered significance has quietly receded from public memory.

This project proposes a work of memory on the Site des Moulins. It is not a museum in the institutional sense, but an open-air sequence of concrete volumes set into the terrain: walls, thresholds, and enclosures that frame the site's existing traces rather than supplant them. The architecture is, in essence, the construction of a ruin, a structure that arrives in the condition of what already surrounds it, refusing the distinction between new and old, between building and remains. Cast-in-place concrete forms belong to the weight and silence of the place, receiving light, weather, and the slow patina of the river's proximity as part of their material life.

The site's history is articulated through water as an ontological thread: from the river as a living territory to be navigated, to a force to be captured, to an industrial infrastructure, to a silenced current beneath parkland. The memorial dimension of architecture is explored through spatial, constructive, and symbolic means: mass and void calibrate the visitor's movement between compression and openness; the monolithic concrete construction echoes the tectonics of the industrial works, binding the new to the logic of the old; and the passage through the sequence—from portage to mill to ruin—is structured as a navigation through successive water ontologies, each threshold marking a transformation in how the site has been conceived through time.

The project is an act of sepulchre: not explanation, but entombment. In other words, a place dense enough to make a long-gone presence felt.
