TITLE: As the Tide Rises
ADVISOR: Michael Jemtrud

Inhabiting the liminal landscapes of coastal retreat

Flooding is the most common and financially destructive natural hazard in North America, responsible for more loss of life and property damage than any other environmental hazard. With the world’s longest coastline, Canada faces widespread vulnerability to coastal flooding, yet responses have often lacked the urgency and coordination required to adequately protect communities. As rising sea levels, intensifying storms, and accelerated coastal erosion converge, many municipalities are now confronting an uncomfortable reality where climate displacement is becoming inevitable.

While displacement carries profound social, economic, and environmental consequences, this project explores how liminal spaces can become catalysts for forms of human and ecological coexistence.

Focusing on the Gaspé Peninsula, with particular attention to the coastal town of Sainte-Flavie, the first phase of the project examines a range of flood resilience strategies to understand the options available to residents and municipalities. These include adaptation, protection, landscaping, relocation, and retreat. These strategies form a framework for evaluating how coastal communities can respond to mounting environmental pressures.

The second phase translates this analysis into a design proposal, that reimagines managed retreat as a phased, community-centered process. Rather than addressing only present-day projections, the project considers longer-term trajectories of climate change and the likely transformation of coastal landscapes over the coming century and beyond. As managed retreat is already underway in parts of Eastern Canada following severe flooding events, the proposal investigates how this process might unfold in ways that preserve community connections while strengthening ecological resilience amid the devastating effects of flooding. As the tide rises, the project asks how the fabric of a community might evolve with it. The design begins by analyzing the components of a city and identifying what can be relocated, what can be deconstructed, and what materials can be reused as part of a local material bank. The residual structures that remain become foundations for new forms of collective space.

Through careful repurposing of salvaged materials and existing infrastructure, the proposal creates a series of community landscapes designed for play, gathering, and ecological regeneration. Elements of former homes and infrastructure transform into spaces for social interaction, evolve into habitats supporting coastal and marine ecosystems. This phased transformation allows communities to inhabit the shifting littoral edge, while honouring the memory of what existed before.
