TITLE: A Black Design Sanctuary
ADVISOR: Samia Henni

What is Black design, and how might it be articulated architecturally? This thesis begins with this question, not as something to resolve, but as something to hold open. Black design is understood here not as a monolith, but as expansive and layered as the Black diaspora itself. To navigate this complexity, the work is organized through three interrelated frameworks: Black Elements, Black Spaces, and Black Architectures.

Black Elements are the gestures, material, aesthetic, and spatial, that carry cultural intention. They express self-definition, communal life, and resistance to white-defaulted norms. Black Spaces are the environments where life unfolds, thresholds, rooms, and gathering places shaped by memory, ritual, and community. Black Architectures emerge from these conditions as spatial expressions of Black existence, imagination, and lived experience.

This research develops a Black Design Encyclopedia and an evolving Black Design Lexicon, positioning Black design as a living, adaptive practice shaped through use and collective making. Through case studies and drawings, it traces how material, sequencing, thresholds, and atmosphere encode memory, dignity, and rhythm. This phase culminates in a proposal for a Black Design Centre, translating the lexicon into built form and asserting permanence in response to the temporary visibility of Black culture.

The work then shifts from definition to embodiment. The project proposes a Black Designer Sanctuary, a space where Black art is not only displayed, but produced, rehearsed, archived, and supported. Conceived as a full creative ecosystem, it brings together studios, performance spaces, media labs, fabrication workshops, galleries, and storage so that making and exhibiting exist in continuous dialogue.

Situated at 153 Dufferin Street in Toronto, along an informal Caribana route, the project engages a site shaped by patterns of Black cultural movement. During Caribana, Black joy fills the city. It is visible, expansive, and undeniable, but only for a moment. When the day ends, that presence recedes. This project asks what it would mean for that presence to become permanent. At its core, the sanctuary is about spatial dignity. It insists that Black art deserves space to be made, preserved, studied, and encountered with intention. It operates as both infrastructure and offering, a place that affirms continuity, presence, and future. A love letter, and an insistence that Black art is enduring, central, and essential.
