TITLE: Make from Waste: A Factory for Bark in Taschereau
ADVISOR: Naomi Keena

Taschereau, in Abitibi—Témiscamingue, is a small municipality of approximately 1,000 residents set within the lakes and boreal forest of northern Quebec. Like many towns in the region, its growth was shaped by the expansion of both the transcontinental railway and forest industry, forces that make Abitibi one of the most resource-productive regions in the province today. Until roughly a decade ago, a sawmill occupied a significant site within the town. As with most mills in Quebec, the operation generated substantial quantities of residual material. Typically, only about half of incoming timber volume leaves a sawmill as dimensional lumber, with the remainder produced as by-products. Bark alone accounts for approximately one third of this residual stream.

The technologies developed to manage bark reveal a characteristically extractive industrial logic. Devices such as the debarker, the beehive burner, and the biomass boiler all frame bark either as waste or, at best, as a short-lived energy fuel. Over time, these technologies reinforced a form of path dependency within the industry, prioritizing efficiency and disposal while concealing the ecological and social costs on which forest production depends. When open burning was restricted and biomass technology was not yet widespread, landfilling became the dominant method for bark disposal in Quebec. In Taschereau, this practice resulted in the accumulation of nearly two million tonnes of bark across multiple landfill pits, part of a broader pattern that includes multiple other sites with similar conditions in neighboring towns. Following the mill’s closure, the buildings were left vacant and the workforce dispersed, yet the material legacy of past production persists. Beneath the surface, buried bark decomposes slowly, producing leachates that interact with soil and groundwater, with potential consequences for protected wildlife habitats, agricultural land, and surrounding forests.

This project reframes the landfilled bark and abandoned sawmill not as liabilities, but as latent resources for a regenerative material production. The buried bark is considered a recoverable substrate that can be transformed into low-carbon construction boards, substituting for gypsum-based and composite finishes. The former mill is reimagined as both a production facility and site of stewardship, supporting the development of local expertise in wood-based materials for construction. What the industry once resolved through efficiency and disposal, the bark stripped, burned, buried, and forgotten, this project asks to hold differently. The proposal for a new factory in Taschereau offers an opportunity to explore alternative systems and infrastructures of resource processing, with architecture acting as a tool to examine the entanglement of landscape, resource, energy, labour, and human experience that make production possible.
