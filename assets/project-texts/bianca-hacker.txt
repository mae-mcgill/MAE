TITLE: Listening to Glacial Memory: Architecture as a Medium for Narrative
ADVISOR: David Theodore

‘Beneath the surface of the glacier, memory gathers within chambers of ice. The glacier is not still, nor silent; it responds and remembers through what is held between spirit, landscape, and time.’

Architectural responses to climate change often frame environmental degradation as a technical problem that can be addressed through sustainable design, infrastructure, or preservation strategies. However, scholarship in environmental humanities, Indigenous studies, and decolonial architecture have increasingly challenged this approach. These perspectives argue that preservationist frameworks are historically entangled with colonial systems of land control, resulting in the displacement of communities and redefining relationships to land. Within this paradigm, landscapes are frequently treated as passive environments to be managed or protected rather than places shaped through ongoing cultural and spiritual relationships.

In particular, glaciers have become central visual indicators of environmental change, as their rapid retreat renders the effects of climate change particularly visible. Beyond scientific and representational roles, glaciers hold deep anthropological and philosophical meaning. Drawing from oral histories of Tlingit and Athabaskan communities in Yukon and Alaska, glaciers are understood as sentient beings that hold memory, respond to human behaviour, and participate in social and spiritual worlds. These stories attribute agency and moral awareness to glaciers, in which glaciers are not passive environments to be preserved but active entities within cultural and ecological systems.

In response, this thesis explores how architectural drawing, and narrative can engage glacial landscapes and cultural narratives without claiming authority over them. The project constructs an imagined architectural world influenced by the glacial landscapes of the Saint Elias region of Yukon and Alaska. The drawings depict narrative forms where listening chambers, basins of memory, and glacial passages become mediums of storytelling and listening. The spaces are represented through large scale sectional, perspectival, and plan drawings, where spatial depth and layering allow them to operate as architectural narrative devices. Accompanying the drawings is a short story that explores the convergence of oral histories, lived environmental knowledge, and fiction. This approach, informed by decolonial perspectives on landscape and narrative-based spatial practice, seeks to reposition architecture as an interpretive and ethical practice. In other words, an architecture that participates in the exchange between memory and land, where not everything is visible at once.
