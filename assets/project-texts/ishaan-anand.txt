TITLE: Designing for Blind Immersion
ADVISOR: Ipek Türeli

Education for visually impaired students (VIS) emerged as a formal practice in the 19th century. Before the founding of Montreal’s Institut Nazareth in 1861, VIS in Canada were barred from attending school, limiting their opportunities. Many educators considered vision as a prerequisite for knowledge, delaying the admission of VIS into formal schooling. In the 20th century, the integrated education movement desegregated specialized institutions, leading to the closure of roughly half of Canada’s schools for VIS. This significantly affected their level of education, as the mainstream schooling system is not equipped with the appropriate learning environments to accommodate the VIS.

Today, about 2.2% of students between the ages of 5 and 14 have a visual impairment and continue to face barriers in mainstream schools. Spaces such as tactile galleries, sensory rooms, and Orientation and Mobility Specialists remain limited, even though they are essential for the development of their spatial and perceptual skills. Despite advances in inclusion policies, outdated infrastructure continues to constrain institutional support for VIS. While newer schools cultivate their environments to respond to diverse learning needs, older institutions remain under resourced, placing additional pressure on teaching staff and reducing opportunities for independence and early educational development among VIS.

This thesis proposes a retrofit of the Ancienne École Allion, an abandoned school in Montreal. The project revitalizes the site and returns it to the blind community of the city. Its program is conceived as a shared learning environment for both visually impaired and sighted students. It fosters space to grow, explore, fall, and get back up again, fundamental to primary education’s play-based learning. The design employs a series of strategies to support autonomy and equitable access to learning for VIS, creating the opportunity to depend on other senses. In the lower primary years, the environment is intentionally saturated in blind specific spatial and sensory cues, providing strong navigational and pedagogical support. As students progress through the school, these cues are gradually reduced, creating a deliberate easing off of guidance systems. This smoothens the transition from a highly supported setting to one that more closely resembles the under-supported conditions students are likely to encounter beyond the school. In this way, the building operates as a scaffolded learning environment, introducing spatial complexity and moments of ambiguity step by step to strengthen independent mobility and decision making skills. The research contributes to the broader discourse on accessible architecture by demonstrating how sensory responsive design promotes autonomy, resilience, and integration for visually impaired learners.
