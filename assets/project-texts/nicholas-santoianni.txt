TITLE: Home, Healing, and the Wait: Designing the In-Between
ADVISOR: Annmarie Adams

The burden of medical displacement creates a hidden crisis within the Canadian healthcare system. Patients travelling to centralized urban hubs like the McGill University Health Centre (MUHC) for specialized care face severe financial burden and psychological trauma during the wait, which is the agonizing period between medical appointments and recovery. The current infrastructure fails many families, particularly those residing in the geographic Crescent of Poverty. Disqualified from philanthropic housing due to rigid radius rules and confronting a systemic lack of affordable temporary housing in the broader city market, these individuals are left isolated and vulnerable.

Home, Healing, and the Wait: Designing the In-Between tackles this logistical and psychological crisis by framing transitional housing not merely as a charitable amenity, but as a vital medical instrument. The project proposes the adaptive reuse of the Trinity Memorial Anglican Church, a 1921 modernized neo-Gothic landmark designed by Ross & Macdonald. Situated seamlessly within Montreal's "Health Corridor," the intervention operates as a hybrid social condenser. The design preserves the church's character-defining stone architecture while densifying the site, effectively bridging the highly medicalized urban context with the local residential fabric of NDG.

To de-institutionalize the patient experience, the design is rooted in the psycho-spatial theory of the "Auxiliary Ego." Clinical environments routinely strip patients and caregivers of their autonomy. To restore this daily agency, the vast, column-free historic nave is preserved and reimagined as a monumental community kitchen, sharing the public realm with the integrated RBC Art and Heritage Centre. Functioning as a spatial evolution of the NDG Food Depot's legacy, this grand communal hearth allows displaced individuals to reclaim their identities through the domestic acts of cooking and sharing meals. By immersing this mutual aid in curated cultural spaces, the architecture embraces normalization. It treats users as active citizens and cultural consumers, offering profound psychological respite that helps postpone the idea of illness.

A new eastern extension provides 50 fully accessible transitional housing units, physically and symbolically connected to the historic sanctuary by a transparent glass threshold. Contrasting the heavy stone of the public church with lighter, warmer materials, the residential spaces adopt an aparthotel typology prioritizing active living over passive sleeping. These units include bariatric, independent, and family suites that feature functional entry buffers, localized kitchennettes for dietary control, and dedicated dining areas for face-to-face connection. Integrated biophilic circulation paths provide crucial sensory relief from sterile clinical environments. Ultimately, the project demonstrates how architecture can transform an isolating medical journey into an experience of community, dignity, and active restoration.
