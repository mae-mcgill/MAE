TITLE: A Space For Sound — Recomposing Spaces of Music Practice in the City
ADVISOR: Ipek Türeli

Montréal is often called a “city of music”. Over the summer especially, thousands gather in its parks, beneath festival stages, and in its venues, drawn by a musical energy the city has learned to celebrate and export. Yet beneath this festive surface lies another dimension, quieter and largely unseen. For every festival performance, there are countless hours of repetition, experimentation, and failure in practice rooms, makeshift studios, borrowed basements, and warehouses where musicians shape their craft. Unlike visual arts or theater, where studios and rehearsal rooms are recognized as legitimate cultural infrastructure, music practice rarely appears in cultural policy, urban planning, or architectural discourse. These spaces form the foundation of everything the city proudly displays, and yet they are vanishing, from both the physical fabric of the city and from any serious account of what sustains its musical identity.

This thesis argues that the city’s relationship to music is extractive, profiting from a culture it does not invest in and celebrating performance while abandoning the conditions that make it possible. Addressing this gap requires building new infrastructure where none currently exists, a civic institution capable of making practice visible, accessible, and structurally supported without erasing the informal culture that has sustained it in the absence of anything better.

The project proposes a civic prototype that takes the informality of existing practice culture not as a problem to be solved but as a starting point to be built upon, developing spatial, programmatic, and governance principles through which a new type of institution becomes possible, one that is publicly owned but non-institutionally operated, acoustically rigorous and culturally grounded, open to anyone while genuinely belonging to the musicians who use it most.

These principles are tested through a design project sited next to the Atwater Market, chosen for its industrial heritage, its proximity to an existing civic market model, and its position within a neighborhood with a strong history of community-led resistance to displacement. The project does not resolve the tension between the informal and the civic but argues instead that this tension is productive and necessary, that a building worth building for music practice must hold both simultaneously without sanitizing one in favor of the other.
