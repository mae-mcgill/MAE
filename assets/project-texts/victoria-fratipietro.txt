TITLE: ModUnité: Reimagining Small Housing Design Processes for Occupant Customization in Montréal-Nord
ADVISOR: Avi Friedman

In Montréal-Nord, where housing affordability and access remain pressing concerns, this project proposes a modular prefabricated housing model grounded in a participatory design framework. Responding to the growing number of households in core housing need, the project reframes affordability not only as a question of cost, but of adaptability; how housing can better reflect the diverse, evolving needs of its occupants.

At the urban scale, the proposal adopts a standard walk-up building typology to balance gentle densification with neighborhood continuity. Within this framework, a range of unit sizes are organized to accommodate varied household types while maintaining construction efficiency. This multiplicity allows the building to respond to demographic diversity without relying on large or resource-intensive dwellings. By working within a repeatable structural and modular system, the project ensures that variation in unit size does not compromise affordability or constructability. Central to the project is the idea that each unit begins as a “blank” yet fully functional spatial framework. Rather than prescribing fixed interior layouts, units are designed with open plans, strategic service cores, and optimized natural light to create flexible, high-quality living environments. From this baseline, a catalogue of interior components, such as partition systems, storage modules, workspace inserts, and kitchen or bathroom components, allows residents to shape their homes according to their needs, preferences, and financial capacity.

This system of interior “filling” positions the homebuyer as an active participant in the design process. Working alongside architects or through guided selection tools, occupants can configure their units at the outset and adapt them over time. This approach acknowledges that households are not static; spaces must be capable of evolving alongside changes in family structure, work patterns, and lifestyle. By enabling incremental customization, the project avoids the limitations of conventional standardized housing while retaining the economic benefits of modular prefabrication.

Ultimately, this participatory model expands the role of architecture in affordable housing. It demonstrates that cost efficiency and design quality are not mutually exclusive but can be aligned through systems that combine standardization with choice. In Montréal-Nord, this approach offers a scalable and inclusive housing strategy, one that empowers residents, supports community diversity, and redefines what it means to live well within a smaller footprint.
