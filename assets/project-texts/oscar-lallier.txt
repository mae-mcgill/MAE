TITLE: Prototyping Forest Resilience
ADVISOR: Michael Jemtrud

Every timber specification is a silvicultural prescription. North American construction’s near-total dependence on spruce-pine-fir (SPF) is not just convenience; it actively embeds monoculture in planted forests. Under-utilized hardwood species are both casualties and solutions to this problem; ecologically indispensable, they provide the functional redundancy, disturbance resistance, and trait diversity that allow forests’ adaptation under harsh climate stress. As temperatures accelerate species redistribution northward and increase pest outbreaks and fires, the homogeneity of harvest-driven forestry amplifies ecological fragility. The construction sector's current strategy toward carbon neutrality is energy-intensive and heavily-processed engineered products like cross-laminated timber from homogeneous SPF, which increases the same dependence as standard light framing. This project argues that material specification is itself an act of forestry. Grounded in Christian Messier's Trait-Based Approach and Peter Osborne's Forest-Building Framework, it establishes that builders, through material choice, exert direct pressure on silvicultural structures.

The research phase develops a “Trait-Based Filter” mapping Quebec species along two axes: ecological traits (growth pattern, succession role, disturbance response, climate adaptability) and material traits (density, modulus of rupture, rot resistance, harvestability). Out of 74 assessed species, five were selected as candidates for implementation in a construction system. These include: Norway maple (Acer platanoides), an invasive and ecologically disruptive species; yellow birch (Betula alleghaniensis), a pioneer of disturbed mixedwood stands; white oak (Quercus alba), a drought-tolerant late-successional hardwood. A catalogue of isolated details links species’ traits to construction applications, forming the foundation for resilient wood-based design.

The design phase materializes these prescriptions into an envelope prototype targeting concrete-like thermal mass. A 200 mm (8”) Norway maple compression wall, with dimensions matching invasive peri-urban growth, was selected for its high volumetric heat capacity. It is assembled with yellow birch dowels, chosen for their high modulus of elasticity, straightness, and cyclic harvest potential. Insulation is provided by 400 mm (16”) of compressed straw bales: an annually regenerating byproduct whose fiber density and low embodied energy complement the hardwood structure. This brings the assembly to U = 0.10 W/m²K, meeting Passive House wall requirements for ASHRAE zone 6A. At 300 kg/m³, the density of the wall is roughly one-eighth of that of concrete. Designed for disassembly, the prototype demonstrates that high performance and ecological coherence are conciliable.
