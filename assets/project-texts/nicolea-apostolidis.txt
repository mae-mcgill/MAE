TITLE: Designing with Daylight
ADVISOR: David Theodore

The Daylight Lab is a theoretical proposition for an architectural environment in which daylight is treated as a primary material to be studied, experienced, and designed with. The Lab operates simultaneously as an instrument, pedagogical space, and architectural experiment, designed to house, manipulate, and reveal daylight in its full temporal and atmospheric range, enabling architects to engage light directly.

The idea emerges from a limitation within contemporary architectural practice. Daylight is predominantly evaluated through quantitative metrics that underpin certification systems. While these frameworks advance environmental accountability, they also produce a reductive understanding of light, separating measurable performance from lived experience. The question of daylight cannot be resolved through numerical thresholds alone, as light is not merely a technical parameter but a physiological, perceptual, and cultural phenomenon.

This disjunction reveals a broader epistemological problem: architecture lacks a coherent framework for integrating the measurable and experiential dimensions of daylight. Simulation tools offer temporal analysis yet fail to capture subtle spatial effects, while intuitive design approaches remain difficult to validate within performance-driven systems. The Daylight Lab is proposed as a direct response to this gap, establishing a controlled yet inhabitable environment where observation, measurement, and design can occur simultaneously, allowing these modes of knowledge to inform one another in real time.

Within the Lab, daylight is approached as inherently programmatic. Different spatial conditions demand different qualities of light, and the building is therefore organized into three interrelated domains: observation, testing, and work. Observation spaces cultivate perceptual awareness, revealing the behavior of light across time, surface, and orientation. Testing environments enable active manipulation through apertures, materials, and geometries, supporting empirical investigation. Workspaces integrate these insights into design production, bridging experimentation with application, and constructing a continuous feedback loop between seeing, measuring, and designing.

The process of designing with daylight is structured through a set of operative principles: solar orientation as a primary generator of form; calibration relative to programmatic needs; the extension and moderation of light through reflection, diffusion, and material articulation; and the conception of spatial boundaries as instruments that shape luminous environments. Rather than prescribing a fixed outcome, the Daylight Lab advances a way of working, reframing daylight as a temporal and generative force that cannot be reduced to metrics, and arguing for a shift from designing for daylight performance towards designing with daylight itself.
